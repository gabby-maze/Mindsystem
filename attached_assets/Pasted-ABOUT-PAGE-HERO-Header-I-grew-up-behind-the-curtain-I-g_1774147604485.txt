HERO
Header: I grew up behind the curtain.
I grew up behind the curtain.
My mother coached volleyball for forty years. And from the time I was old enough to sit in a gym I was watching — not from the stands like everyone else — but from the inside. I saw the hours she put in that nobody ever noticed. I heard the conversations coaches have when parents aren't in the room. I watched what happened when programs overpromised and underdelivered. I saw the gap between what families were told and what was actually happening.
I sat on the bus with the athletes. I heard what they said to each other when the adults weren't listening. I watched young women carry pressure that nobody was acknowledging and perform confidence they didn't actually feel.
I saw the whole ecosystem — the athlete, the parent, the coach, the program — from an angle most people never get access to.
And then I became the athlete.
I earned a Division I scholarship to UC Berkeley and stepped onto the court carrying everything I had watched and absorbed for years. I was competitive. Assertive. Resilient. On that court I knew exactly who I was.
And then the sport ended. And I walked into the rest of my life carrying a system that was built entirely around outcomes — and nothing else.
The career. The title. The next level. I chased every external marker I could find because that's what the system had taught me to do. Not because anyone failed me. Because those questions — who are you becoming, what do you want, what do you stand for — were never part of the equation to begin with.
It took me seventeen years in high performance sales — and decades of quietly working through an identity I didn't fully recognize as my own — to understand what had happened.
The system I grew up in developed what I could produce. It never developed who I was.

SECTION 2: THE REALIZATION
Header: The system wasn't broken. It was built for someone else.
Here's what I know now that took me decades to see clearly.
The system that youth sports was built on — outcomes, performance, winning, production — was not designed with your daughter in mind. It was designed for a world that rewarded a very specific kind of person. And that person was not her.
We live in a society that has been conditioning girls from birth to be smaller. Softer. Quieter. More agreeable. To take up less space. To make other people comfortable at the expense of their own voice. To smile through the hard parts. To be likeable before they are respected. To shrink themselves to fit into rooms that were never built for them.
That conditioning doesn't announce itself. It whispers. In the way we praise girls for being nice instead of being right. In the way we reward compliance over conviction. In the way we teach them to apologize before they speak and ask permission before they lead.
And it compounds. Quietly. Over decades.
Sport is one of the only places in your daughter's life where that conditioning gets interrupted.
On the court she is not asked to be smaller. She is coached to be bigger. Louder. More assertive. More competitive. She is taught to fail publicly and come back. To lead when it's uncomfortable. To take up every inch of space available to her and then demand more. To compete without apology.
Those are not just athletic skills. Those are survival skills for a world that will spend the rest of her life asking her to be less than she is.
The court is where she learns to fight back against everything society is quietly teaching her to accept.
But only — only — if someone teaches her to see it that way.
If all we do is focus on the outcome — the playing time, the ranking, the scholarship — we hand her the medal and send her back into a world that will immediately start asking her to shrink again.
And she will. Because nobody showed her what she was really building on that court.
I was that girl.
I don't want your daughter to be.


SECTION 3: WHO I AM NOW
Header: I walked through fire to get here. I'm building the shortcut so she doesn't have to.
I am the most authentic version of myself that I have ever been.
It took me until my forties to say that. And I had to walk through fire to get there.
For the last five years I have been unbuilding a life that was constructed by a former version of me. A version that was trained to chase outcomes, perform confidence, and measure worth by external markers. A version that was good at fitting into rooms that weren't built for her because she had spent a lifetime learning how.
Most people I know would not have taken the risks I have taken. Would not have faced the accountability I have faced. Would not have made the shifts I have made. Because unbuilding a life you worked hard to construct — even when you know it isn't really yours — is one of the hardest things a person can do.
But I did it. Because I had to. And because on the other side of it was the person I had been on that court all along — just finally living off it too.
And then I had daughters.
Twin girls. Fourteen years old. Competing at the highest levels of club volleyball in our region. Moving through the exact same system I grew up in. Being shaped by the exact same forces I was shaped by.
And I made a decision.
The cycle stops here.
I don't want my daughters to get to 43 and ask themselves why it took so long to find out who they were. I don't want them to spend decades performing a version of themselves that looks like winning on the outside while quietly losing on the inside. I don't want them to unbuild their lives the way I unbuilt mine.
I want them to build it right the first time.
Here's what I learned along the way that changed everything.
I started this work with mothers. Women trying to find themselves on the other side of motherhood — after the career pivots, the identity sacrifices, the years of quietly putting themselves last and calling it love. And what I discovered broke my heart a little.
Most of them wouldn't do the work.
Not because they didn't want to. But because they had already been so deeply conditioned to sacrifice themselves for the people around them that they had convinced themselves they were okay with it. That it was their job. That wanting something just for themselves — time, a hobby, a sense of self that belonged only to them — was somehow selfish.
They were not okay. They just didn't have permission to say so.
So I went upstream.
If the conditioning starts early — and it does — then the interruption has to start early too. Before the patterns calcify. Before the identity gets handed over piece by piece to everyone else's expectations. Before she spends decades unbuilding what was never really hers.
This is my gift to the younger generation.
Be ten. Be twelve. Be exactly where you are — fully, unapologetically, without rushing toward the next thing.
Just be it intentionally.
Notice what lights you up and what drains you. Notice how you feel after a great practice versus a hard loss. Notice the moments when you feel most like yourself — and the moments when you don't. Document the process. Know where you are in relation to where you have been. Define your standards and practice upholding them.
Because the young woman who has been building that self awareness since she was ten — who has been paying attention to who she is while she is becoming her — does not get to forty three and wonder where she went.
She already knows.
That is what MindSystem is built to do.


SECTION 4: MY PHILOSOPHY
Header: Everyone's been talking about it. This is what you do about it.
There is no shortage of conversations about youth athlete mental health. About confidence. About pressure. About the importance of process over outcome. About balance and burnout and how to raise resilient kids in a high stakes sports environment.
The conversation has been happening for years.
What hasn't existed — until now — is a system that actually does something about it.
Not a podcast episode you listen to on the way to practice. Not a book you read and feel inspired by and then put down. Not a therapist your athlete sees once a week who has never sat on a bench before a big match. Not another camp or clinic that addresses her skills and sends her back to the same family environment unchanged.
A system.
Something your whole family moves through — together. With a shared language. A shared process. A shared way of navigating the moments nobody prepares you for.
MindSystem is not built around awareness. Awareness is where most programs stop. MindSystem is built around action. Specific. Repeatable. Season long action that develops who your athlete is — not just what she produces.
This is not therapy. This is not coaching. This is not another thing to add to the schedule.
This is the infrastructure your family has been missing.
The athlete who moves through this process doesn't just perform better. She knows herself better. She knows what she stands for. She knows how to reset when things go wrong. She knows how to communicate with the adults in her life. She knows — at ten, at twelve, at fourteen — who she is becoming.
And the family around her? They stop guessing. They stop reacting. They stop absorbing each other's stress without knowing why.
They start guiding. Together. From inside the process.
That is what MindSystem does.
That is what nothing else has done.
